Introduction and Importance of Tajweed

Tajweed, the set of rules governing the proper pronunciation and recitation of the Quran, plays a pivotal role in ensuring a profound connection between the reciter and the sacred text. The importance of Tajweed is multifaceted, encompassing various aspects that contribute to a more meaningful and accurate engagement with the Quran.

Firstly, Tajweed serves as a safeguard against errors in Quranic recitation. The recitation of the Quran is expected to be flawless, and the application of Tajweed rules becomes instrumental in achieving this perfection. This is particularly crucial when imparting the knowledge of the Quran to children at a young age, as it lays the foundation for them to recite efficiently and without mistakes.

Beyond mere precision, Tajweed is indispensable for the correct interpretation of the verses. The rules of Tajweed guide the reciter on when to start, stop, or pause during the recitation, thereby influencing the cadence and rhythm of the delivery. This meticulous attention to the natural pauses and flows of the text ensures that the meanings of the verses are conveyed accurately. Tajweed becomes a means to breathe life into the words of the Quran, allowing for a more profound understanding of its message.

Furthermore, Tajweed contributes to a better comprehension of the Quran, especially when reciting from a hard copy that includes various signs representing Tajweed rules. Without a grasp of these rules, reciters may find themselves uneasy or confused during the recitation. Familiarity with Tajweed, therefore, becomes imperative for a smoother and more immersive experience with the sacred text.

Additionally, Tajweed delves into the intricacies of pronunciation and phonetics. Each Arabic letter has a distinct sound, and Tajweed rules further categorize them based on the touch of the tongue and the part of the throat involved in producing the sound. This nuanced understanding of individual letters, as well as their combinations, enhances the reciter's ability to articulate each word with precision and clarity.

The obligation of Tajweed is emphasized through the teachings of the Prophet Muhammad (SAW). He demonstrated a deliberate and measured approach to Quranic recitation, adhering to the rhythm as instructed by Allah. The Prophet's emphasis on beauty and melody in Quranic recitation is evident in his teachings, where he encouraged reciting in a pleasant and melodious tone. The Prophet's compliments to those who recited with feeling underscore the importance of infusing emotion and sincerity into the recitation, as opposed to a rushed and toneless delivery.

In conclusion, Tajweed is not merely a set of rules but a profound discipline that enriches the reciter's connection with the Quran. It serves as a guide for precision, interpretation, comprehension, and pronunciation, all of which contribute to a more meaningful and spiritually fulfilling engagement with the sacred text.

Sides of tounge

The instructor elucidated the intricate articulation points, known as makhârij, essential for proper pronunciation in Arabic. These points are categorized into five primary locations: the oral cavity (al jawf), the throat (al-ḥalq), the tongue (al-lisân), the lips (al-shafatān), and the nasal passage (al-khayshūm).

The oral cavity (al jawf) plays a significant role in articulating specific elongation letters, including ا, و, and ي, each producing distinct sounds when accompanied by a sukoon and a specific vowel. These letters are referred to as al huruf al hawa’iyya due to their airy nature, with pronunciation ceasing only when breath runs out.

The throat (al-ḥalq) encompasses three exit points: aqsâ al-ḥalq (back of the throat), wasat al-ḥalq (middle of the throat), and adnâ al-ḥalq (entrance to the throat). From these points, letters like hamza (ء) and hâ (ه) are articulated.

Near the glottis, situated at the pharynx, the letters ‘ayn (ع) and Hâ (ح) are pronounced. The region closer to the mouth, producing letters ghayn (غ) and khâ (خ), requires emphasis due to their strong nature.

The lecture emphasizes the importance of recognizing these articulation points for accurate pronunciation, illustrating examples from the Holy Qur'an. Understanding the specific locations for each letter enhances the learner's grasp of Arabic phonetics and aids in precise and meaningful recitation.

Articulation point

In this lecture, the instructor delves into the intricate articulation points of the tongue, lips, and nasal cavity in Arabic pronunciation, providing a comprehensive understanding of the makhârij. The tongue, a versatile organ, contributes to 18 letters through ten distinct articulation points:

1. The farthest part of the tongue against the pharynx and upper palate produces the letter qâf ق.
2. Just below qâf, where the upper part of the tongue meets the upper palate, kâf ك is articulated.
3. The middle of the tongue meeting the upper palate produces the letters djîm ج, shîn ش, and yâ ي (as a consonant).
4. The sides of the tongue pressing against the upper premolars and molars articulate the unique dâd ض.
5. One edge of the tongue's tip against the palatal mucosa provides the pronunciation for the letter lâm ل.
6. Slightly above lâm's position, the tongue's tip presses the palatal mucosa, articulating the letter noûn ن.
7. Just below noûn's point, where the tip presses against the palatal mucosa causing the tongue's rear to vibrate, is the articulation point for râ ر.
8. The tongue's tip against the palatal mucosa of the upper central incisors articulates the letters ta ت, dâ د, and Tâ ط.
9. The tip, combined with the part separating the lower central incisors, shapes the sounds for sâd ص, shîn ش, and zey ز.
10. By resting the tip of the tongue against the tip of the upper central incisors and slightly protruding it between the teeth, the letters Zâ ظ, dhâl ذ, and thâ ث are pronounced.

The lecture categorizes these articulation points based on the deepest part, the deepest part and what lies opposite, the middle part, the tip of the tongue, the lower part of the tip, the top side of the tongue's tip and the gums of the front two upper incisors, the tip of the tongue and the gums of the front upper incisors, the tip of the tongue and the edge of the front two lower incisors, and the tip of the tongue and the bottom edge of the front two upper incisors.

Additionally, the lips serve as articulation points for fâ ف, while the space between the lips forms sounds for wâw و, ba ب, and meem م.

The nasal cavity contributes to the characteristic nasal resonance or ghunna, notably present in the pronunciation of noon ن and meem م with a shaddaّ over them, or when noon ن, meem م, or tanween is in a state of sukoon under specific conditions.

In summary, the lecture provides a detailed exploration of the articulation points for various Arabic letters, enhancing learners' understanding of phonetics and pronunciation intricacies.

Tanween

In this lecture, the intricacies of Tanween, a diacritical mark in Arabic, are thoroughly explored, focusing on its impact on pronunciation and its transformation in certain linguistic scenarios. Tanween, when applied, introduces an "n" sound to the end of a word, a phenomenon referred to as "nunation." The circumstances under which Tanween occurs include double Fatha (Nasb), double Kasra (Jar), or Dhamma (Raf).

The lecture emphasizes the two main scenarios regarding the pronunciation of Tanween: when it is attached to the next letter, it is pronounced, but if there is a pause on Tanween, it is not read. This distinction is vital for accurate and contextually appropriate Arabic pronunciation.

The stances of Tanween within a word are then discussed, shedding light on its varied applications. An example is provided to illustrate how Tanween operates in a practical linguistic context.

An essential aspect covered in the lecture is the unique transformation that Tanween undergoes when a pause is made after a word ending with Tanween Al-Fatha. In this case, Tanween is converted into an Alif and is halted by lengthening an amount of Alif. However, a crucial exception is highlighted: this transformation does not occur if the last letter of the word is a Taa Marbutah (ة).

In summary, the lecture provides a comprehensive understanding of Tanween, elucidating its role in nunation, offering practical examples, and emphasizing its nuanced pronunciation rules, including its transformation in specific linguistic scenarios. This knowledge is fundamental for learners aiming for precision and accuracy in Arabic pronunciation.

Huroof e Maddaat and Maddaat

In this lecture, the focus is on the Tajweed rules related to Madd (elongation), specifically centered around the three Madd letters: ا (Alif), و (Waw), and ي (Ya). The concept of Madd involves stretching or extending the sounds of these letters.

1. Madd Types:
- Wow Madd and Alif Madd: The stretching of a sound for two counts occurs when there is a Damma before a wow sakin (Wow Madd) or a Fatha before Alif (Alif Madd). Examples like "نو حیہ" demonstrate the application of Wow Madd and Alif Madd.


2. Types of Madd:
- Natural Madd (Madd Asli/Tabi’y):** This involves the natural elongation of vowels with the Madd letters.
- Secondary Madd (Madd Far’ee):** Dependent on the presence of Hamza or Sukoon, and includes subtypes like Madd A’red Li-Ssukoon, Madd Al-Leen, Madd Wajeb Muttasil, and Madd Ja’ez Munfasil.

3. Madd A’red Li-Ssukoon:
- Temporary Madd for stopping.
- Prolongation of Madd Tabee’ee (Natural Madd) when a sakin letter follows at the end of a word due to stopping.

4. Madd Al-Leen:
- Applies to Leen letters (A, Waw, or Ya’ Sakin preceded by a letter with a Fatha).
- Prolongation if a Leen letter is followed by a sakin letter at the end of a word due to stopping.

5. Madd Wajeb Muttasil:
- Mandatory elongation when a Madd letter is followed by a Hamzah in the same word.

6. Madd Ja’ez Munfasil:
- Permitted elongation when a Madd letter at the end of a word is followed by a Hamzah at the beginning of the next word.

7. Madd Lazem:
- A necessary prolongation of Madd letters.
- Two main types: Kalimi Muthaqqal (heavy) and Kalimi Mukhaffaf (light).
- Further categorized into Madd Lazem Harfee Muthaqqal and Madd Lazem Harfee Mukhafaf, each involving a 6-beat prolongation.

Huroof Leen

In this lecture, the focus is on the concept of "Leen" in Tajweed, which means to read softly. This soft reading applies to two specific letters: و (Waw) and ي (Ya). The lecture highlights the condition under which this soft reading occurs.

1. Leen Letters:
- Leen involves reading the letters و (Waw) and ي (Ya) softly.

2. Ya Leen:
- Occurs when there is a ي (Ya) with sukoon, and the letter before it has a fatha.
- The soft reading of Ya in this context is referred to as "Ya Leen."

The lecture emphasizes the subtleties of pronunciation in Tajweed, highlighting the importance of reading certain letters softly under specific conditions. This concept contributes to the overall precision and beauty of Quranic recitation.

Huroof e Qalqala

In this lecture,the focus is on "Huroof Qalqala" in Tajweed, which refers to the echoing or shaking sound produced when certain letters have a Sukoon symbol on them or when a pause occurs at these letters. The lecture highlights the specific letters categorized as Qalqala and the variations of Qalqala intensity based on their positions in the Quranic verses.

1. Qalqala Letters:
- The Qalqala letters are ق (Qaaf), ط (Taa), ب (Baa), ج (Jeem), and د (Dall).

2. Qalqala Categories:
- Qalqala Kubra (Strong Echoing): Occurs at the end of an Ayah (verse).
- Qalqala Wusta (Medium Echoing): Occurs at the end of a word in the middle of an Ayah.
- Qalqala Sughra (Light Echoing): Occurs in the middle of a word, whether at the beginning, middle, or end of an Ayah.

3. Intensity Variation:
- The strength of Qalqala is determined by the position of the word within the verse. It is strong at the end, intermediate or medium in the middle, and weak if it occurs while continuing to the next letter.

4. Noticing Qalqala:
- Qalqala is noticeable when the Qalqala letters have Sukoon. If there's a vowel, it is pronounced normally without the echoing or gurgling sound.

5. Qalqala Rules:
- Qalqala is emphasized when pausing at the end of each verse, especially for strong Qalqala.
- The last letters requiring Qalqala may not have Sukoon but should be pronounced with a strong Qalqala when at the end of a verse.

This lecture provides a comprehensive understanding of the concept of Qalqala, its specific letters, and the variations in its intensity based on the position of words within Quranic verses.

Rules of Raah

The lecture on the Rules of Raa (ر) delves into two main types of pronunciation: the heavy Raa (Tafkheem Of Raa) and the light Raa (Tarqeeq Of Raa), depending on the diacritics (Harakahs) on Raa. The summary encapsulates the key points:

1. Types of Raa:
- There are two main types of Raa pronunciation: heavy (Tafkheem) and light (Tarqeeq). The determination is based on the diacritics present on the letter Raa.

2. Tarqeeq of Raa:
- Raa is pronounced with Tarqeeq in several scenarios:
- When Raa is followed by Kasrah (ــــــِ).
- When Raa has Sukoon and is preceded by a letter with Kasrah.
- When Raa has Sukoon and is preceded by Yaa Sakinah.
- When Raa has Sukoon and is preceded by a sakin letter, which is preceded by a letter with Kasrah.

3. Tafkheem of Raa:
- Raa is pronounced with Tafkheem in specific situations:
- When Raa has Sukoon and is preceded by Dammah.
- When Raa has Sukoon and is preceded by a sakin letter, which is in turn preceded by a Dammah.
- When Raa has Sukoon and is preceded by Hamzatul-Wasl.
- In five specific words: رْ – صَ – قَ – طَ

4. Priority for Tarqeeq and Tafkheem:
- While both lightness and heaviness are allowed for Raa, the lecture highlights specific cases where Tarqeeq or Tafkheem takes precedence. Tarqeeq is favored in five Quranic words, while Tafkheem is prioritized in only one word (مِـــــصْـرَ).

The comprehensive summary provides a clear understanding of the rules governing the pronunciation of the Arabic letter Raa based on its diacritical marks.

Rumooz e Auqaf

The lecture on the Rules of Rumooz e Auqaf (Stopping Signs) in the Quran emphasizes the significance of proper pauses and breaks while reciting the Holy Scripture. The summary captures the essential points:

1. Introduction:
- Every language has specific rules for communication, and the Divine Language of the Holy Quran has its own principles.
- Proper pauses aid in understanding and reciting the Quran with correct Tajweed, rhythm, and phonetics.

2. Waqf e Taam (Perfect Stop):
- Represents the finishing end of a Quranic verse.
- Indicated by a circle at the conclusion of a line.
- Requires the reciter to stop, take a breath, and fully grasp the meaning before proceeding.

3. Waqf e Laazim (Imperative Stop):
- A sign that demands the reader to stop, as not pausing here can drastically alter the meaning of the sentence.

4. Waqf e Mutlaq (General Stop):
- Indicates a pause in recitation, allowing the reader to take a breath and discontinue for a brief period.
- Eases the reading process by absorbing the meaning of the already read text.

5. Waqf e Jaaiz (Permissible Stop):

- Points to the completion of the discussed matter in that part of the Ayah.
- Not obligatory but helps in absorbing the discussed meaning before moving on.

6. Waqf e Mujawwaz (Allowance to Continue):
- Signifies that there is no need to pause, and the recitation can continue.

7. Waqf e Murakh-khas (Conditional Stop):
- Permits a break and breath if feeling tired, but it's advisable to continue reading.

8. Al-wasl Awlaa (Continue Recitation):
- Indicates to continue recitation without stopping.

9. Qeela ‘Alayhil-Waqf (Optional Stop):
- Suggests not stopping the recitation, though opinions on stopping may vary.

10. Qad Yusal (Permission to Continue):
- Specifies permission to continue reading, but one may choose to stop here.

11. Waqf e Manzil (Stop Signaling Angel's Pause):
- Sign indicating Angel Jibrael's pause during the revelation of Quranic instructions to the Holy Prophet ﷺ.

12. Waqf-un-Nabi (Prophet's Stop):
- Sign indicating parts of the Quran where the Messenger ﷺ Himself stopped and took a pause.

The summary encapsulates the importance and types of stopping signs in the Quran, guiding readers on when and how to pause for a deeper understanding of the text.
